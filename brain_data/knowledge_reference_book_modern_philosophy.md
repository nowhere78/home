# 📖 Modern Philosophy: A Comprehensive Reference Guide

> **Era:** 17th Century to Early 20th Century
> **Domain:** Epistemology, Metaphysics, Political Philosophy, Existentialism
> **Purpose:** To provide a high-level overview of the major thinkers and movements that shaped the Western philosophical tradition from the Renaissance to the Modern era.

---

## 1. The Epistemological Divide: Rationalism vs. Empiricism
The central debate of early modern philosophy: *How do we acquire knowledge?*

### Rationalism (Reason is the primary source of knowledge)
*   **René Descartes (1596–1650):** The father of modern philosophy. He utilized "Methodological Skepticism" (doubting everything that can possibly be doubted) to find a single, unshakeable truth.
    *   *Cogito, ergo sum:* "I think, therefore I am." He could doubt his body and his senses, but he could not doubt that he was doubting.
    *   *Dualism:* The strict separation of the immaterial mind and the material body.
*   **Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677):** Argued for Pantheism—that God and Nature are the exact same thing. Everything is governed by absolute logical necessity; free will is an illusion.
*   **Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716):** Proposed that the universe is made of infinite, indivisible, non-interacting spiritual points called "Monads."

### Empiricism (Sensory experience is the primary source of knowledge)
*   **John Locke (1632–1704):** Argued the mind is a *Tabula Rasa* (blank slate) at birth. All ideas come from experience and reflection. (Also a foundational thinker for classical liberalism and property rights).
*   **David Hume (1711–1776):** A radical skeptic. He argued that we cannot logically prove causality (just because the sun rose yesterday doesn't logically guarantee it will rise tomorrow; it's just a habit of mind). He stated that "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions."

---

## 2. The Synthesis: Immanuel Kant (1724–1804)
Kant revolutionized philosophy by synthesizing Rationalism and Empiricism.
*   **The Copernican Revolution of Philosophy:** Kant argued that the mind does not passively record the world (like a camera); rather, the mind actively *structures* the world. We can only experience the world through the built-in "software" of our minds (Space, Time, and Causality).
*   **Phenomena vs. Noumena:** We can only ever know the *Phenomenal* world (the world as it appears to us). We can never know the *Noumenal* world (the "thing-in-itself," the world as it exists completely independently of human perception).

---

## 3. 19th Century: Idealism and Materialism

### Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831)
*   **Absolute Idealism:** Reality is a historical process of "Absolute Spirit" (Geist) coming to know itself through human history.
*   **The Dialectic:** History progresses through a pattern of thesis (a starting idea) → antithesis (its contradiction) → synthesis (a higher truth that resolves the conflict).

### Karl Marx (1818–1883)
*   **Dialectical Materialism:** Marx flipped Hegel upside down. He argued that it is not *ideas* that drive history, but *material economic conditions* and class struggle.
*   **Alienation:** Under capitalism, workers are alienated from the products of their labor, from the act of producing, from their own human nature, and from each other.

---

## 4. Late 19th/Early 20th Century: The Rise of Existentialism
A rebellion against massive, abstract philosophical systems (like Hegel's), focusing instead on the concrete, lived experience of the individual.

### Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855)
*   The father of Existentialism. He focused on individual anxiety, dread (Angst), and the necessity of making a "leap of faith" into the absurd to find meaning.
*   *"Subjectivity is truth."*

### Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)
*   Proclaimed "God is dead," meaning the Enlightenment had destroyed the absolute moral foundation of Christianity. He warned this would lead to nihilism.
*   He called for the *Übermensch* (Overman) to create their own values through the "Will to Power," rather than submitting to "herd morality."

### Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)
*   **Existence precedes essence:** Unlike a knife (which is designed with a purpose *before* it is made), humans simply exist first. We have no pre-determined essence or purpose. We must define our own essence through our actions.
*   **Radical Freedom:** We are "condemned to be free." We are entirely responsible for our choices. Blaming determinism or human nature is "bad faith" (*mauvaise foi*).
