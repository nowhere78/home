# 🩸 Survival Medicine & First Aid Field Guide

> **Domain:** Wilderness Medicine, Austere Environment First Aid, Trauma Care, Herbal Medicine
> **Purpose:** To provide a comprehensive, actionable reference for treating trauma, illness, and injury in situations where modern medical infrastructure is inaccessible (the "Golden Hour" has passed, and rescue is not coming).

---

## 1. Core Philosophy of Austere Medicine
In a normal urban environment, first aid is simply keeping the patient alive until the ambulance arrives. In a survival scenario, **you are the ambulance, the emergency room, and the long-term care facility**.

### The MARCH Protocol (Trauma Assessment)
Replacing the old "ABC" (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) protocol, MARCH is the standard for tactical and wilderness trauma care. You must treat these in exact order; there is no point clearing an airway if the patient bleeds to death in 60 seconds.

1.  **M - Massive Hemorrhage:** Stop catastrophic arterial bleeding immediately (Tourniquets, wound packing).
2.  **A - Airway:** Ensure the windpipe is clear of obstructions (blood, vomit, tongue). Use recovery position or airway adjuncts.
3.  **R - Respiration:** Check for chest injuries (e.g., tension pneumothorax/sucking chest wound). Seal open chest wounds.
4.  **C - Circulation:** Check for signs of shock and lesser bleeding. Elevate legs, keep the patient warm.
5.  **H - Hypothermia/Head Injury:** Strip wet clothing, insulate the patient. Blood does not clot well when the body is cold.

## 2. Managing Catastrophic Bleeding

### Tourniquets (TQs)
*   **When to use:** For bright red, spurting (arterial) blood on a limb that cannot be stopped with direct pressure.
*   **Application:** Place "high and tight" on the limb (above the joint, not on a joint). Tighten the windlass until the bleeding *completely stops* and the pulse below the TQ is gone.
*   **Myth-busting:** A properly applied TQ can remain in place for 2–4 hours with minimal risk of amputation. Do not loosen it periodically.

### Wound Packing
*   **When to use:** For deep junctional wounds (groin, armpit, neck) where a tourniquet cannot be applied.
*   **Application:** Shove hemostatic gauze (or regular clean cloth) directly *into* the wound cavity, finding the severed artery and maintaining heavy, direct pressure for 3–5 minutes.

## 3. Infection Control & Wound Care
In a survival situation, a minor cut can become a lethal systemic infection (sepsis).

*   **Irrigation is King:** The single most important step in wound care is pressure irrigation. Wash the wound out with copious amounts of the cleanest water available. Do not pour raw iodine, alcohol, or hydrogen peroxide *into* a deep wound, as it kills healthy tissue and delays healing. Use those to clean the *intact skin around* the wound.
*   **Closure:** Do not stitch or superglue a puncture wound or a dirty wound closed. This traps bacteria inside and guarantees an abscess. Leave dirty wounds open to heal from the bottom up (packing them with sterile dressing changes).
*   **Honey:** Raw, unpasteurized honey has incredible natural antibacterial properties and creates an osmotic barrier. It was heavily used in ancient (including Viking and Egyptian) wound care.

## 4. Environmental Injuries

### Hypothermia (Cold)
*   **Symptoms:** Uncontrollable shivering (mild), cessation of shivering and confusion (moderate), paradoxical undressing and unconsciousness (severe).
*   **Treatment:** Remove wet clothing immediately. Provide a vapor barrier (tarp/space blanket) and a thermal barrier (sleeping bag/wool). Skin-to-skin contact in a sleeping bag is highly effective. Feed them warm, sugary liquids if conscious.

### Hyperthermia (Heat Stroke)
*   **Symptoms:** Throbbing headache, confusion, nausea, and crucially, *the cessation of sweating* (the skin becomes hot and dry).
*   **Treatment:** This is a life-threatening emergency. Rapidly cool the patient by any means necessary (dousing with water, fanning, ice packs in armpits/groin). Do not force them to chug water if vomiting.

## 5. Austere Herbalism & Natural Remedies
When the pharmaceutical supply chain fails, knowledge of medicinal plants is vital. *Warning: Requires absolute 100% positive identification.*

*   **Willow Bark (Salix spp.):** Contains salicin, the chemical precursor to aspirin. Can be chewed or boiled into a tea for pain relief, fever reduction, and anti-inflammatory action.
*   **Yarrow (Achillea millefolium):** A legendary "woundwort" used by soldiers from Achilles to the Vikings. The crushed leaves applied to a wound act as a powerful styptic (stops bleeding) and mild antiseptic.
*   **Sphagnum Moss:** Naturally highly acidic and incredibly absorbent. Used extensively during WWI as a sterile field dressing for severe wounds when cotton ran out.
*   **Plantain (Plantago major):** A common broadleaf weed. Chewing the leaf into a "spit poultice" and applying it to insect stings, bites, or minor cuts rapidly draws out toxins and reduces swelling.
*   **Pine Sap / Pitch:** Naturally antibacterial and waterproof. Can be used as an emergency liquid bandage to seal minor cuts from dirt.
