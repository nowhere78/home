# ᛟ The Old Norse Language and Runic Alphabets: A Reference Guide

> **Domain:** Linguistics, Old Norse, Futhark Runes, Germanic Philology
> **Purpose:** To provide a comprehensive reference for the language of the Vikings and the runic alphabets they used to record it.

---

## 1. The Old Norse Language (Dönsk tunga)
Old Norse was the North Germanic language spoken by the inhabitants of Scandinavia and their overseas settlements from about the 9th to the 13th centuries.

### Dialects
It was not a monolithic language, but rather a continuum of dialects:
*   **Old West Norse:** Spoken in Norway, Iceland, Greenland, and the settlements in the British Isles. The vast majority of surviving Old Norse literature (the Sagas, the Eddas) is written in Old Icelandic, a very conservative form of Old West Norse.
*   **Old East Norse:** Spoken in Denmark and Sweden. It had different vowel shifts and simplifications compared to West Norse.
*   **Old Gutnish:** Spoken on the island of Gotland.

### Linguistic Characteristics
*   **Highly Inflected:** Like Latin or modern Icelandic, nouns, pronouns, and adjectives decline into four cases (Nominative, Accusative, Dative, Genitive), three genders (Masculine, Feminine, Neuter), and two numbers (Singular, Plural).
*   **U-Umlaut:** A distinct phonological feature where an 'a' in the root syllable shifts to an 'ö' if there is a 'u' in the following syllable (e.g., *land* -> plural *lönd*).
*   **Post-posited Definite Article:** Instead of a separate word for "the" (like English), Old Norse attaches the article to the end of the noun. (e.g., *hestur* = a horse; *hesturinn* = the horse).

## 2. The Runic Alphabets (The Futhark)
Runes were the writing system of the Germanic peoples before the adoption of the Latin alphabet. They are characterized by straight lines and sharp angles, designed to be easily carved into wood or stone along the grain.

### The Elder Futhark (c. 150 – 800 AD)
The oldest runic alphabet, used by the proto-Germanic tribes prior to the Viking Age.
*   **Structure:** Consists of 24 runes, traditionally divided into three *ættir* (families) of 8 runes each (Freyr's ætt, Heimdall's ætt, Tyr's ætt).
*   **Usage:** Mostly used for short inscriptions on jewelry, weapons, and early runestones. Often associated with magic and invoking the gods.
*   *Note:* The Vikings of the 9th century *did not* use the Elder Futhark.

### The Younger Futhark (c. 800 – 1100 AD)
This is the true alphabet of the Viking Age.
*   **Structure:** Paradoxically, as the Old Norse language gained *more* vowel sounds, the alphabet was simplified from 24 down to only **16 runes**.
*   **Ambiguity:** Because there were fewer runes than spoken sounds, many runes did double or triple duty. For example, the rune *Úr* (ᚢ) could represent 'u', 'o', 'y', or 'v'. The rune *Kaun* (ᚴ) could represent 'k' or 'g'. Reading Younger Futhark requires significant contextual guesswork.
*   **Variants:**
    *   *Long-branch runes:* More formal, used for carving into stone (runestones).
    *   *Short-twig runes:* A "cursive" shorthand used for everyday carving on wood.

### The Mythological Origin of Runes
According to the *Hávamál* (in the Poetic Edda), runes were not invented by humans, but discovered by Odin.
*   Odin hung himself from the branches of Yggdrasil, pierced by his own spear, for nine days and nine nights, fasting and staring into the abyss below.
*   Through this ecstatic ordeal, he perceived the magical, underlying structures of reality—the runes—and "took them up screaming."

## 3. Notable Runic Terms & Translations

*   **Rún:** Meaning "secret," "whisper," or "mystery."
*   **Galdr:** Magic or incantation, often sung.
*   **Seiðr:** A form of shamanic magic involving entering a trance state to discern or alter fate (wyrd), typically practiced by women (*völur*).

### Common Phrases
*   *Til Valhallar* (To Valhalla)
*   *Drengskapr* (Honor/Courage/Manliness)
*   *Innangarð / Útangarð* (Inside the enclosure / Outside the enclosure - civilized vs. chaotic space).
