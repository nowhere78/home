# 📐 Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus: Comprehensive Reference

> **Author:** Ludwig Wittgenstein
> **Published:** 1921
> **Domain:** Analytic Philosophy, Philosophy of Language, Logic, Epistemology

---

## 1. Context and Core Thesis
The *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* is one of the most important philosophical works of the 20th century. Written by Wittgenstein while he was a soldier in the trenches of World War I, it is the only book he published during his lifetime. 

**The Core Thesis:** Wittgenstein attempts to identify the absolute limits of language and, therefore, the limits of thought. He argues that philosophical problems (like those concerning ethics, metaphysics, and religion) are not actually problems to be solved, but rather linguistic confusions resulting from a misunderstanding of how language works. 

His goal is to show what can be said clearly (the facts of natural science) and to demonstrate that everything else (ethics, aesthetics, the meaning of life) cannot be spoken about logically, but only "shown."

---

## 2. Structure of the Text
The book is famous for its rigorous, mathematical structure. It consists of seven main propositions, with sub-propositions branching off as decimal numbers (e.g., 1, 1.1, 1.11, 1.12). 

The seven main propositions are:
1.  **The world is everything that is the case.**
2.  What is the case (a fact) is the existence of states of affairs.
3.  A logical picture of facts is a thought.
4.  A thought is a proposition with a sense.
5.  A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions.
6.  The general form of a truth-function is: [p, ξ, N(ξ)]. This is the general form of a proposition.
7.  **Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.**

---

## 3. Key Concepts

### The Picture Theory of Language
Wittgenstein argues that language works by painting logical "pictures" of the world.
*   A proposition (a statement) has a structure that mirrors the logical structure of a state of affairs in the real world.
*   Just as a map is a spatial picture of a terrain, a sentence is a logical picture of a fact.
*   Therefore, language can only meaningfully describe facts (empirical reality). 

### Logical Positivism
The *Tractatus* became the foundational text for the Vienna Circle and the movement of Logical Positivism, which asserted that a statement is only meaningful if it is either a logical tautology (like "2+2=4") or empirically verifiable.

### The Mystical (What Cannot Be Said)
Because language can only picture facts in the world, it cannot step *outside* the world to talk about the world as a whole.
*   **Ethics and Aesthetics:** Cannot be expressed in logical propositions. They are "transcendental." You cannot find "good" or "evil" under a microscope; therefore, you cannot speak about them logically.
*   **The Meaning of Life:** "The solution of the riddle of life in space and time lies outside space and time."
*   These things are not meaningless; in fact, they are the most important things in human life. But they can only be *shown* through action and art, not *said* in logical philosophical arguments.

## 4. Notable Quotes for Retrieval

> *"The world is everything that is the case."* (Proposition 1)

> *"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."* (Proposition 5.6)

> *"Death is not an event in life: we do not live to experience death. If we take eternity to mean not infinite temporal duration but timelessness, then eternal life belongs to those who live in the present."* (Proposition 6.4311)

> *"My propositions serve as elucidations in the following way: anyone who understands me eventually recognizes them as nonsensical, when he has used them—as steps—to climb up beyond them. (He must, so to speak, throw away the ladder after he has climbed up it.)"* (Proposition 6.54)

> *"Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."* (Proposition 7)

---

## 5. Wittgenstein's Later Turn
Remarkably, decades later, Wittgenstein completely rejected the core premises of the *Tractatus*. In his posthumously published *Philosophical Investigations*, he abandoned the "Picture Theory" in favor of "Language Games." He realized that language is not just for stating logical facts, but is a fluid, social tool used for countless purposes (giving orders, telling jokes, praying), and its meaning derives from its *use* in a specific context, not its logical mapping to reality.
