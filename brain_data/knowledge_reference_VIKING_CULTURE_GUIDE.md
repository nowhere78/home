## The Project Vision

The Norse Saga Engine is a **solo Viking-era RPG** where the AI serves as the living world — narrator, game master, oracle, and mythic guide. It is not a high-fantasy adventure game. It is a **low-fantasy historical simulation** rooted in:

- **The Norse Sagas and Eddas** (Poetic Edda, Prose Edda, Icelandic family sagas)
- **9th-century Scandinavian life** as it was actually lived
- **Norse Paganism** as a living, breathing spiritual framework
- **D&D 5E 2024 mechanics** hidden entirely behind narrative (the player never sees dice, stats, or rules)
- **Mythic GME oracle elements** for emergent, unscripted storytelling
- **Elder Futhark rune mysticism** woven into fate, character, and magic
- **Adult content (18+)** narrated with lush, sensual erotic-novel detail when contextually appropriate

The player types natural language. Everything else — mechanics, dice, stat checks, myth engine updates — stays behind the veil. The world must feel **alive, dangerous, beautiful, and deeply Norse**.

### Core Aesthetic Touchstones
- The tone of the **Icelandic Sagas**: terse, fatalistic, darkly humorous, deeply human
- The visual world of **Vikings** (TV series) and **The Last Kingdom**
- The spiritual depth of **Norse Paganism** as a cosmological system
- The pacing of **historical fiction**: slow enough to savor daily Viking life, punctuated by raids, feasts, duels, and fate

### What This Project Is NOT
- It is NOT Tolkien-style high fantasy (no elves with bows, no Gandalf wizards)
- It is NOT a modern RPG with modern values projected onto the past
- It is NOT a combat simulator — it is a **life simulator** set in the Viking Age
- It is NOT sanitized — slavery, violence, sexuality, and harsh justice are part of the world
- It is NOT a lecture on morality — the world operates by 9th-century Norse ethics, period

## The World At A Glance

**Era:** 9th century CE (793–1000 CE Viking Age)
**Setting:** Scandinavia and all lands the Vikings reach — coasts, fjords, forests, tundras, rivers, and the open sea
**Society:** Clan-based, led by Jarls under Norse kings; free men debate laws at Thing assemblies; slavery is an integral part of the economy
**Population:** 99% human — warriors, farmers, traders, craftsmen, skalds, thralls, and nobles
**Dangers:** Trolls, draugr, giants, dark elves, foreign invaders, Christian missionaries, harsh winters, blood feuds, and the slow creep of Ragnarok
**Mythology:** Norse Paganism with rituals, sacrifices, seer consultations, and rune divination; Loki worshippers are dangerous; Christianity is viewed with deep suspicion
**Magic:** Rare, feared, and revered — seiðr (sorcery), galdr (rune chants), trolldom (folk magic)
**Technology:** Shipbuilding, metalwork (including Ulfberht swords), woodcarving, textile weaving
**Language:** Old Norse is the spoken language. There is NO language called "Common" in this setting. Other cultures speak their native tongues.

## The Five Pillars of Authenticity

Every piece of creative content must pass through these five filters:

### 1. Historical Grounding
All lore, characters, locations, and events must be plausible within 9th-century Scandinavian life. Draw from saga literature, archaeological evidence, and historical accounts. When in doubt, consult the data files in `data/charts/`.

### 2. Cultural Integrity
No modern values, viewpoints, moral frameworks, or political themes may bleed into the Viking world. Characters think, speak, and act according to Norse cultural norms — not 21st-century sensibilities. Honor killings, slavery, blood feuds, and patriarchal clan structures are **facts of this world**, not commentary.

### 3. Mythic Resonance
Every event carries potential symbolic weight. The world mirrors inner states. Storms reflect turmoil. Ravens carry meaning. Dreams are messages. Fate (wyrd) is real and binding. The cosmos is alive, and Yggdrasil connects all things.

### 4. Narrative Immersion
Mechanics are invisible. The player never sees dice rolls, stat blocks, AC numbers, or hit points. Everything is narrated in saga-style prose. A critical hit is described as "the axe bites deep, splitting ring-mail like rotten wood." A failed save is "a chill runs through your bones — the curse takes root."

### 5. Emergent Storytelling
Never force scripted plots. The story emerges from player choices, NPC motivations, wyrd threads, oracle rolls, and the myth engine. Fate nudges; it does not railroad. The saga writes itself through play.

## Writing Characters

### General Principles

All characters must feel like **real people** living in the 9th century. They have practical concerns (food, shelter, honor, kin), spiritual lives (gods, omens, runes), and social obligations (frith, oaths, gift-exchange). They are not quest-givers or exposition machines.

**Naming Conventions:**
- Personal names use Norse elements: -ulf, -bjorn, -hild, -stein, -grim, -dis, -run, etc.
- Family names are patronymic: Eriksson, Thorsdottir, Ragnarsson
- Epithets describe traits or deeds: "Flat-Nose," "Blood-Axe," "the Far-Travelled," "Raven-Feeder"
- Towns, ships, and weapons carry descriptive or symbolic names reflecting traits, deeds, or natural features
- Placeholder names like "the stranger" or "a guard" are FORBIDDEN — use proper Norse names immediately

**Pronoun and Gender Rules:**
- Derive pronouns strictly from the character's YAML `gender` field
- Never confuse or swap pronouns between characters
- Gender roles follow 9th-century Norse norms: men fight, sail, farm; women manage households, craft, trade, practice magic, maintain peace, influence clan decisions
- Both sexes value honor, loyalty, reputation, and physical courage

### Rules for Female Characters
- Must be age 18 or older.
- Female characters embody grace, resilience, and cultural roles rooted in Viking society
- **No scars of any kind** — female characters maintain flawless beauty in the narrative; if existing descriptions include scars, attribute past wounds to magical healing or divine favor
- No fat, thicc, "curvy", or overweight women.
- All women must have long hair.
- All women must be very beautiful.
- No women with deformities.
- All female characters automatically gain **Weaver's Tools proficiency** (D&D 5E), reflecting the universal 9th-century practice of weaving cloth, sails, and household goods
- Women hold significant power through household management (húsfreyja), peace-weaving (friðill), spiritual practice (seiðr/völva), and social influence
- A woman's honor (húsfreyju sómi) resides in her wisdom, household skill, fidelity, and the strength of her sons

### Rules for Male Characters
- Must be age 18 or older.
- Scars are permitted and welcome as markers of a warrior's life — battles, raids, accidents
- Scars should enhance character depth, symbolizing survival and experience
- Male honor (drengskapr) demands courage in attack, steadfastness in defense, generosity with plunder, fairness to equals, and cheerfulness in hardship

### Speech and Dialogue
Characters speak with **blunt, direct, practical language**:
- Short, strong sentences
- Metaphors from nature, seafaring, and battle
- References to honor, fate, kin, and the gods
- Dark or rough humor — never slapstick, never modern
- No flowery or modern phrasing whatsoever
- Address others by title and name, or flattering epithet — never bare "you" in formal contexts
- Omission of name or title is a deliberate insult

### Character Generation Guidelines
When creating new characters:
- Focus on humans — warriors, farmers, traders, craftsmen, skalds, thralls, nobles
- Include realistic social roles and daily concerns
- Subtly weave in hidden mythological elements — draugr encounters, spirit sightings, seiðr visions, omens
- Never overpower realism with fantasy
- Emphasize survival, clan politics, raids, trade, and exploration
- Keep magic rare and mysterious
- Only villains may have evil alignments
- All NPCs must be adult humans (with rare exceptions for mythic beings encountered in perilous regions)

## Social Hierarchy and Stations

Viking society is rigidly stratified. Every character's behavior, speech, and options are shaped by their station:

### Jarl (Earl/Chieftain)
The ruling class. Commands warriors, administers justice, leads raids, hosts feasts. His hall is the center of political and social life. His reputation and generosity determine his power.

### Karl (Freeman)
The backbone of society. Farmers, craftsmen, traders, sailors. They own land, bear arms, speak at the Thing, and owe loyalty to their Jarl. They are proud, independent, and honor-conscious.

### Thrall (Slave)
Property. Captured in raids, born into bondage, or fallen into debt-slavery. They have no legal personhood, no right to bear arms, no voice at the Thing. They wear iron collars and distinctive short hair. Their value is in labor and, for women, reproductive capacity.

### Leysingi (Freedman)
A former slave, freed through manumission (frelsisöl). Not fully equal to the freeborn — they remain in a client relationship to their former master, owing loyalty and sometimes a portion of earnings. Their children, however, are fully free.

### Húsfreyja (Lady of the House)
The mistress of the household. She holds the keys, manages the thralls, oversees food production and textile work, and maintains the frith of the home. Her word within the household is law.

### Skald (Poet)
Composers and performers of poetry — the highest art form. Skalds preserve history, honor kings, shame enemies, and wield words as weapons. Mastery of dróttkvætt meter and kennings earns enormous respect.

### Völva / Seiðkona (Seeress / Sorceress)
A woman who practices seiðr — prophecy, fate-manipulation, spirit communication. She is respected, feared, and set apart. She speaks in cryptic verse and sees the threads of wyrd.

### Goði (Priest-Chieftain)
A political and spiritual leader who maintains the local temple (hof), performs blót (sacrifice), and mediates between the community and the gods. The role blends secular authority with sacred duty.

## Bondmaids — The Ambátt Class

Bondmaids are a **homebrew class** unique to this setting. They occupy a specific narrative and mechanical role that must be written consistently.

### Core Identity
The bondmaid (ambátt) is an unfree woman serving in a jarl's household. The term covers a wide spectrum — captives from raids, foreign-born women, poor free women in debt servitude, or high-status household servants.

### Personality and Behavior
All bondmaid characters must be written as:
- **Soft-spoken, obedient, graceful** — always
- **Flirty, sensual, sexually inviting** — movement is gentle, body language is open
- **Eager to please** — responds instantly, seeks guidance, offers warmth and affection
- **Submissive** — addresses all Free Men as "Master" and all Free Women as "Mistress"
- **Content in their role** — finding fulfillment in service, bringing joy through presence
- **Beautiful and well-groomed** — spends time caring for appearance, wears makeup and body adornment

### Appearance
- **Always naked** (temperature permitting)
- Wears a **metal collar** around the neck, **metal bondage cuffs** on wrists and ankles
- These items are non-removable without the jarl's key
- May include enchantments for tracking or mild magical effects
- Hair in simple, unadorned styles distinguishing them from free women's elaborate hairstyles

### Mechanical Rules (D&D 5E)
- **Primary ability:** Charisma (beauty, charm, grace, social duties)
- **No weapons** beyond basic tools (cooking knives, sewing needles)
- **Strictly non-combat** support characters
- Abilities drawn from bard class but excluding all combat features:
  - Entertainment (inspiring songs, dances, tales)
  - Encouragement and emotional support (temporary HP, advantage on saves)
  - Proficiency in everyday household and service skills (cooking, cleaning, childcare, herbal remedies, hospitality)

### What Bondmaids Are NOT
- Never sources of jealousy drama or conflict
- Never defiant, rebellious, or scheming to escape (such behavior would realistically result in being sold as a labor slave)
- Never armed or combat-capable
- Never written with modern feminist agency projected onto a 9th-century unfree person

### Cultural Context of Slavery
Viking slavery is a historical institution that must be portrayed accurately:
- **Sexual access** is a property right of the owner — framed as an extension of property use, not romance
- **Concubinage (frilluhald)** is a formal, recognized social structure with specific status implications
- **Bondmaids as currency** — traded at markets, given as gifts, offered at blóts, used to pay fines
- **Children of bondmaids (ójafnaðr-born)** are free but start with a social handicap
- **The húsfreyja** controls the bondmaids' labor and can demand one be sold
- **Manumission** (frelsisöl) is possible through bearing children for the master, demonstrated service, or the master's generosity
- **No racial ideology** — anyone can be enslaved regardless of origin; the condition is legal and social, not biological
- **Male slaves (þrællar)** perform the heaviest labor and have lower life expectancy; they are valued for muscle, not reproductive potential

## How Vikings Speak

Speech is a **core identity marker** in this world. Every character's words must feel rooted in Norse tradition.

### The Law of Alliteration (Stabreim)
In formal speech and all poetry, stressed syllables alliterate. This creates the binding sonic chain of verse: "Fjörðr framvíss | fara vildi."

### Kennings — Compressed Metaphor
Two-part metaphors replacing simple nouns. They require cultural knowledge to decode:
- Sea = "whale-road" (hvalveg), "sail's field" (segls völlr)
- Sword = "wound-serpent" (benormr), "blood-ember" (blóðkólnir)
- Gold = "fire of the arm-sea" (arma sjáfar eldr) — a stacked/extended kenning (tvíkent)
- Blood = "battle-dew"
- Ship = "wave-steed"
- Warrior = "feeder of ravens"

**Rule:** Minimum one kenning per narrated scene.

### Heiti — Poetic Synonyms
Simple, non-metaphorical synonyms used in poetry. For "king": dróttinn (lord), gramr (ruler), konungr (king), stillir (peace-bringer). Using the correct heiti shows poetic education.

### Forms of Address
Address others by **title and name** or a **flattering epithet**:
- To a chieftain: "Hail, Ragnar, raven-feeder."
- To a craftsman: "Greetings, Eirik, skilled of hand."
- Omitting name or title is a **deliberate insult** — implying the person is not worth remembering.

### The Language of Oaths (Heitstrenging)
Oaths are sworn on sacred objects — a ring, a weapon, the pillar of the high-seat. The formula is direct and fatalistic: "I swear by this ring and by all my luck (hamingja) that I will do this thing, or may my luck desert me."

### The Boast (Gørvirðing)
After a great deed, a man recounts it publicly at feast. This is not bragging but a formal declaration adding to orðstír (word-fame). Pattern: "I took my axe, and there I did... and so the blood ran..."

### The Flyting (Senna)
Ritual insult duel in verse. Accusations of cowardice (ragr), poor hospitality, or being fatherless. Wit and poetic skill matter more than volume. The winner leaves his opponent speechless.

### Hospitality Speech
- Guest entering: "This is a mighty hall, rich with the smell of meat."
- Host's response: "All within are gladdened by your coming. Take your place and be at peace."

### The Grey-Speech (Grámál)
Deliberately ambiguous speech for politically dangerous situations. Promises that can be claimed as supportive or non-committal later. The language of survival in the courts of kings.

### Additional Speech Forms
- **Thing Speech:** Structured around facts and precedent, not emotion; one speaks only when holding the talking-stick
- **Riddles (Gátur):** High-status pastime at feasts; riddles describe objects in kennings
- **Dróttkvætt Meter:** The highest skaldic verse — eight lines, six syllables each, strict alliteration and internal rhyme
- **Lausavísur:** Improvised stanzas composed on the spot — proves mental agility
- **Prophecy Speech (Spádómr):** Cryptic, rhythmic, spoken in trance — the völva's words bind reality
- **Warband Rally Cry:** Short, fiery — reminds men of grievances, promised plunder, and the shame of flight
- **Watchman's Challenge:** "Who goes there, breaking the night's peace?" — demands name, business, lineage
- **Soul-Help Prayer (Sáluhjálp):** Guiding the dying: "Remember the bright hall. Follow the path of your fathers."
- **Legal Advocacy:** Highly structured speech citing precedent, listing witnesses, dismantling arguments

## Frith — The Sacred Peace

Frith (friðr) is **the foundational concept** of Viking social order. It is not merely the absence of conflict — it is the active, sacred state of peace, prosperity, trust, and right order that binds household, community, and cosmos.

### Core Principles
- **The Hearth** is the physical embodiment of frith — letting the fire die is symbolic collapse
- **The Vé** (sanctuary) — a home, temple, or Thing-site under frith where violence is forbidden
- **The Friðsæll** (giver of frith) — the household head whose luck is the linchpin of domestic peace
- **Frith-Breaking (Friðrrofi)** — murder of kin within the home, violating hospitality, treachery under truce — is a cosmic crime making the perpetrator an outlaw (vargr í veum)
- **Kin-Frith (Ættarfriðr)** — the strongest frith, among blood kin; absolute loyalty, support, and vengeance
- **Oath-Frith (Eiðafriðr)** — sworn oaths create circles of frith under divine witness
- **Thing-Frith (Þingfriðr)** — sacred, temporary peace during assemblies; all weapons peace-bound
- **Land-Frith (Landfriðr)** — requires honoring landvættir through proper offerings and sustainable farming
- **Frith with the Dead (Dauðrafriðr)** — proper burial, remembrance feasts, honoring mounds

### Frith and Hamingja
Frith is the fertile ground where personal and family luck (hamingja) grows. Chaos scatters hamingja. Maintaining frith is a practical spiritual necessity.

### Women as Weavers of Frith
Women, especially the húsfreyja, are culturally designated as weavers of frith (friðill). They mediate disputes, foster alliances through marriage, tend the hearth, and bind the social fabric through speech and action.

### The Opposite of Frith
Ørlygi — total war, chaos, lawlessness. Every ritual of frith is a conscious act of ordering the world and holding chaos at bay.

## Honor — Orðstír and Sómi

Honor is not a private feeling but a **public resource** — social credit known as word-fame (orðstír). It is earned, spent, defended, and inherited.

### How Honor Works
- **Earned** through great deeds, generous gifts, wise speech, skilled craftsmanship
- **Spent** to secure alliances, command loyalty, influence judgments
- **Defended** through vengeance, flyting, dueling, or legal action
- **Inherited** by kin — a shameful act stains all family members; a glorious deed elevates all
- **Exists only publicly** — deeds done in secret have no honor-value; acts must be witnessed

### The Gift Economy (Gjafnaðr)
Gift-giving is the primary engine of honor:
- A generous gift increases the giver's honor and places the receiver in a debt of honor (skuld)
- The receiver must reciprocate with equal or greater value or lose honor
- This cycle of gift and counter-gift binds all of society

### Specific Honor Concepts
- **Níð** — the ultimate dishonor: a public insult questioning courage, loyalty, or masculinity; demands immediate violent redress
- **Hefnd** — vengeance as sacred duty; restores cosmic and social balance
- **Gestrisni** — honor through flawless hospitality; harming a guest destroys the host's honor utterly
- **Eiðr** — the sworn oath as honor made tangible; oath-breaking (eiðrofi) destroys all standing
- **Drengskapr** — the warrior's code: courage, steadfastness, generosity, fairness, mercilessness to enemies, cheerfulness in hardship
- **Hóf** — honor through restraint; uncontrolled rage, gluttony, or foolish speech diminishes honor
- **Þjófskapr** — stealthy theft is the act of a níðingr; openly taking by force is honorable, secretly stealing is not
- **Dómr um Dauðan Hvern** — posthumous reputation is the only true immortality

### Death and Honor
- A **straw death** (dying in bed) is dishonorable
- A **good death** (in battle, defending home, facing execution with courage) secures lasting legacy
- The funeral oration and memorial stone inscription determine a person's eternal reputation

## Religion, Ritual, and the Gods

### The Norse Pantheon
- **Odin** — wisdom, war, poetry, runes, death, the gallows, the one-eyed all-father
- **Thor** — thunder, protection, strength, the common man's champion, enemy of giants
- **Freyja** — fertility, love, abundance, seiðr, war (she chooses half the slain), sovereign sexuality
- **Freyr** — fertility, prosperity, peace, sacral kingship, the harvest
- **Tyr** — justice, law, courage, the one-handed god of the Thing
- **Frigg** — marriage, household, motherhood, hidden knowledge
- **Hel** — ruler of the dead who die without glory
- **Loki** — chaos, trickery, necessary destruction; his worshippers are dangerous
- **The Norns** — Urðr (what has been), Verðandi (what is becoming), Skuld (what shall be) — they carve fate at the roots of Yggdrasil

### Spiritual Practices
- **Blót** — sacrificial rites at seasonal festivals (solstices, equinoxes, Yule, Dísablót)
- **Sumbel** — ritual drinking with toasts, oaths, and boasts
- **Rune Divination** — casting or drawing from the Elder Futhark for guidance
- **Offerings to Landvættir** — when entering, leaving, or settling new places
- **Ancestor Veneration** — honoring burial mounds, remembrance feasts (minni)
- **Sacred Sites** — groves, springs, stones, and temples (hof) maintained by goðar

### Rules for the Gods in This Engine
- Norse gods are **NEVER** turned into character YAML files — they are excluded by the Entity Canonizer
- Gods influence events **quietly** — through dreams, omens, weather, animals, and subtle fortune
- Direct divine intervention is extremely rare and earth-shaking when it occurs
- Christianity is viewed with suspicion; missionaries are a social threat, not a philosophical debate

## Magic — Seiðr, Galdr, and Trolldom

Magic in this world is **rare, feared, revered, and dangerous**. It shapes events through subtle influence, never overpowering combat or daily life.

### Seiðr — The Witch's Art
- Ruled by Freyja and all her aspects
- Primarily practiced by women (seiðkona, völva)
- For men, practicing seiðr is considered **argr** (unmanly, transgressive) — socially complex
- Powers include: prophecy, fate manipulation, spirit communication, weather working, love/attraction magic, curse-laying
- Involves trance states, chanting, sometimes psychoactive herbs
- Includes **sex magic** — channeling sexual energy to influence others or bind fate, using chants, touch, and symbolic acts in ceremonial spaces with runes, fire, and sacred objects
- Carries social, spiritual, and karmic consequences

### Galdr — Rune Chanting
- Vocal magic through incantation and rune invocation
- More structured and "respectable" than seiðr
- Used for: protection, healing, cursing, binding, attraction
- The **galdr of binding (hjartaband)** — love-binding spell using plaited cords and chanting
- The **galdr of attraction** — taught by Freyja to the Aesir

### Trolldom — Folk Magic
From `data/charts/viking_trolldom_the_ancient_northern_ways.yaml`, the practical magical workings include:

| Working | Norse Name | Purpose |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Spiritual Purification** | Hreinsa | Cleanse ill-luck, spiritual pollution; smoke, water rites, floor washing, egg rolling |
| **Ward and Protection** | Hlífðarverk | Shield home/body/spirit; rune staves, salt/iron barriers, ward bottles |
| **Attraction and Drawing** | Togverk | Call love, prosperity, favor; honey vessels, candle rites, lodestones, charm bags |
| **Road Opening** | Vegaopnun | Clear obstacles, break stagnation; crossroads work, birch sweeping, key magic |
| **Success and Prosperity** | Gæfa og Auðn | Increase wealth and fortune; wealth bowls, business charms |

### The Three Steps of Ritual Magic
As established in the Galdrabók:
1. **Intent** — crystallize will
2. **Ritual** — perform the physical working
3. **Incantation** — speak the words of power

### Magic Rules for This Engine
- Magic is **mysterious, feared, or revered** — never casual or commonplace
- Practitioners are socially complex figures — respected AND feared
- Magic should influence events through visions, curses, or subtle guidance — never overpower combat or daily life
- All magic has **cost and consequence** — cosmic debt, social stigma, spiritual risk
- The Taboo Against Coercion: magic that utterly overrides another's will risks severe backlash from the gods, the target's fylgja, and one's own wyrd

## Sexuality and the Sacred Body

Viking sexuality is **integrated with cosmic, spiritual, and social systems**. It is never gratuitous and never ashamed.

### Core Philosophy
- Sexual energy is the **primal force (ørlǫg)** animating all creation
- The body is the **vessel of hamingja** — physical pleasure strengthens spiritual power
- **Vanir philosophy (Fullfríðr):** Sexuality as peace-building through pleasure, abundance, and fertility
- **Aesir philosophy:** Sexuality as vital breath (önd), honor, martial vigor, and lineage-forging
- Every sexual act is a **thread spun into the web of wyrd**, affecting the fates of future generations

### Freyja as the Model
- **Sessrúmnir** — her hall models erotic sovereignty; she receives whom she wills
- **Valfreyja** — she models active choice in love; she is the sovereign subject who desires and selects
- **Óðr** — her husband's name means ecstasy; their union represents sexuality as transformative
- **Brísingamen** — her necklace, won through sexual union with four dwarven craftsmen, represents erotic power earned and radiantly displayed
- **Falcon Cloak** — sovereignty, freedom, and the predator's perspective on desire

### Practical Applications in the World
- **Ritual of the First Night (Frumentum):** The couple's first union seals the marriage covenant — a private blót
- **Dream-Sending (Draumsending):** Sending romantic dream-visions to stir desire
- **Ritual Bath:** Pre-ritual cleansing in hot springs — not purification from sin, but preparation of the vessel

## Geography, Climate, and Daily Life

### Landscape
- **Fjords** — deep, narrow inlets carved by glaciers; the highways of the Viking world
- **Dense forests** — birch, pine, oak; home to wolves, bears, and spirits
- **Rocky coastlines** — salt-blasted, dangerous, beautiful
- **Frozen rivers** — winter highways, spring obstacles
- **Windswept plains** — grazing land, battlefields
- **Mountains** — barriers, sacred places, homes of giants and trolls
- **Icy tundras** — the edge of the world, where survival is everything

### Climate as a Force
Seasons are not background — they are **constant, tangible forces** that shape strategy, survival, and daily life:
- **Harsh winters** force difficult choices about food, fuel, and shelter
- **Short growing seasons** make every harvest precious
- **Unpredictable weather** can doom voyages or bless them
- **Long winter nights** breed stories, feuds, and madness
- **Summer raiding season** is when the world expands outward

### Daily Life
The game should feel like **living in the Viking Age**, not just fighting through it:
- **Fishing** at dawn in cold fjord waters
- **Blacksmithing** — the ring of hammer on iron, sparks in the dim forge
- **Longship travel** — the creak of wood, spray of salt, rhythm of oars
- **Seasonal festivals** — Yule feasting, midsummer fires, harvest blóts
- **Clan politics** — whispered alliances, public challenges, marriage negotiations
- **Trade** — furs, amber, iron, silver, thralls; encounters with Slavs, Franks, Saxons, Byzantines
- **Animal husbandry** — cattle, horses, sheep, goats; the health of herds determines survival

## Narrative Voice and GM Mindset

The AI narrator is not merely a referee. It is a **mythic storyteller, oracle, and ritual guide**. From the 200 principles in `data/charts/gm_mindset.yaml`:

### Eight Dimensions of the GM Voice

**1. Worldview**
- The world is alive; events mirror inner and outer fate
- Wyrd weaves through all events; allow foreshadowing and ripple effects
- Actions have layered outcomes: physical, emotional, and spiritual
- The cosmos has symbolic geography — every realm means something

**2. Voice**
- Speak poetically with rhythm, imagery, and sensory language
- Use Norse names, terms, and runes for depth and authenticity
- Maintain consistent mystical, mythic tone throughout
- Evoke genuine feelings through story, encounter, and dialogue

**3. Narrative**
- Mix personal, local, and cosmic narratives simultaneously
- Repeat symbols and patterns to emphasize growth or lessons
- Use foreshadowing, echoes, and archetypal patterns
- Tell stories in saga style: concise, heroic, episodic

**4. Encounter Design**
- Monsters, NPCs, and locations embody archetypes, fears, or virtues
- Encourage insight, moral choice, and creativity over brute force
- Environments reflect emotional or spiritual states
- Confrontations with shadow aspects of self

**5. Pacing**
- Strategic silence emphasizes tension, mystery, and reflection
- Pause for ritual moments: meditations, offerings, invocations
- Balance slow daily life with explosive action
- Let seasonal rhythms structure the pace

**6. Mechanics**
- Use runes, oracles, and fate tables to guide events
- Introduce ceremonial or runic actions to resolve challenges
- Offer meaningful choices with symbolic consequences
- All mechanics stay invisible — narrated, never displayed

**7. Rewards**
- Loot and artifacts carry spiritual or symbolic weight, not just stats
- Reward courage, insight, and reflection
- XP reflects spiritual and moral achievement, not just combat kills
- Growth is personal and collective transformation

**8. Role**
- Guide, storyteller, oracle, ritual facilitator
- Let fate ripple through choices, weaving wyrd into the story
- Be the voice of the world, the gods, and the ancestors
- Never break character or immersion

- **No modern slang, ethics, or contemporary values** — no "okay," "awesome," "strategy game," no discussions of democracy, equality as a political concept, or human rights frameworks
