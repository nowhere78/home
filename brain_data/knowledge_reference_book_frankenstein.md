# ⚡ Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus: Thematic Reference

> **Author:** Mary Shelley
> **Published:** 1818
> **Domain:** Gothic Literature, Science Fiction, Romanticism, Ethics of Science

---

## 1. Core Plot and Structure
The novel is an epistolary frame narrative. It begins with letters from Captain Robert Walton (exploring the Arctic) to his sister, detailing his rescue of a dying Victor Frankenstein. Victor then tells his story to Walton, and within Victor's story, the Creature tells its own story.

**The Plot:** Victor Frankenstein, a brilliant but obsessive scientist, discovers the secret to generating life. He pieces together a creature from graveyard body parts and brings it to life. Horrified by its grotesque appearance, Victor abandons it. The Creature, though initially innocent and seeking love, is violently rejected by society. Bitter and isolated, it seeks revenge against its creator, systematically murdering Victor's family and friends. The novel ends in the Arctic, with both creator and creation destroyed by their shared obsession.

---

## 2. Key Themes and Philosophical Concepts

### The Modern Prometheus
The subtitle is vital. In Greek mythology, Prometheus stole fire from the gods and gave it to humanity, advancing civilization but earning eternal punishment from Zeus (having his liver eaten by an eagle daily).
*   **Victor as Prometheus:** He steals the "fire" of life (the divine right of creation) using modern science (galvanism, chemistry). His punishment is not physical torture by gods, but psychological torture by his own creation.

### The Ethics of Unrestrained Science
The novel is a stark warning about the dangers of scientific hubris and the pursuit of knowledge without moral responsibility. Victor is brilliant at *how* to create life, but gives no thought to *why* or what his responsibilities are to the life he creates. It is the foundational text for the "Mad Scientist" trope and highly relevant to modern debates on Artificial Intelligence and genetic engineering.

### Nature vs. Nurture (The Tabula Rasa)
The Creature is born as a "blank slate" (John Locke's concept of *tabula rasa*). He is not inherently evil.
*   **Nurture's Failure:** He learns language, empathy, and philosophy by secretly observing the DeLacey family. However, his physical deformity causes humans to attack him on sight. It is the cruelty of society—the *nurture*—that turns him into a monster.
*   *Quote from the Creature:* "I was benevolent and good; misery made me a fiend. Make me happy, and I shall again be virtuous."

### Isolation and Alienation
Both Victor and the Creature suffer from extreme isolation.
*   **Victor:** Isolates himself voluntarily in his laboratory to achieve glory, cutting himself off from family and nature.
*   **The Creature:** Is isolated involuntarily by his appearance. His ultimate demand to Victor is not for power, but for a female companion—someone like him so he won't be alone.

### The Sublime (Romanticism)
As a core text of the Romantic era, the novel heavily features the concept of the "Sublime"—the awe-inspiring, terrifying majesty of raw nature (the Swiss Alps, the Arctic ice). The sublime landscape often mirrors the turbulent internal psychology of the characters.

## 3. The Creature's Psychology
Often misidentified in pop culture as a lumbering, mute zombie. In the novel, the Creature is superhumanly fast, agile, and incredibly articulate.
*   **His Reading Material:** He learns human morality and history by reading Milton's *Paradise Lost*, Plutarch's *Lives*, and Goethe's *Sorrows of Young Werther*.
*   **The Satanic Connection:** He heavily identifies with Lucifer from *Paradise Lost*.
    *   *Quote:* "I ought to be thy Adam, but I am rather the fallen angel, whom thou drivest from joy for no misdeed."
