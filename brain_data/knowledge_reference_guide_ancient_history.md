# 🏛️ Ancient History: A Comprehensive Reference Guide

> **Domain:** Antiquity, Early Civilizations, Bronze Age, Iron Age
> **Purpose:** To provide a high-level, accurate historical reference of the major civilizations of the ancient world, their contributions, and their timelines.

---

## 1. The Cradle of Civilization: Mesopotamia
Located in the fertile crescent between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers (modern-day Iraq).

### Sumer (c. 4500 BC – c. 1900 BC)
*   **Key Contributions:** The invention of the wheel, the first known writing system (**Cuneiform**), the 60-minute hour, and the 360-degree circle.
*   **City-States:** Uruk, Ur, Eridu. These were independent cities with their own patron deities and ziggurats (stepped temple towers).
*   **Literature:** *The Epic of Gilgamesh*, the oldest surviving piece of notable literature.

### Babylon (c. 1894 BC – 539 BC)
*   **Hammurabi (r. 1792–1750 BC):** Famous for the **Code of Hammurabi**, one of the earliest and most complete written legal codes ("an eye for an eye").
*   **Neo-Babylonian Empire:** Under Nebuchadnezzar II, Babylon became the largest city in the world, known for the (potentially mythic) Hanging Gardens and the Ishtar Gate.

## 2. Ancient Egypt
A civilization built entirely around the life-giving floods of the Nile River.

### The Old Kingdom (c. 2700 – 2200 BC)
*   **The Age of the Pyramids:** The construction of the Great Pyramid of Giza under Pharaoh Khufu. This period was marked by absolute divine kingship and massive monumental architecture.

### The Middle Kingdom (c. 2050 – 1652 BC)
*   A period of cultural and literary flourishing. The pharaohs were seen less as inaccessible gods and more as shepherds of the people.

### The New Kingdom (c. 1567 – 1085 BC)
*   **The Empire:** Egypt expanded its borders aggressively.
*   **Key Figures:** Hatshepsut (one of the most successful female pharaohs), Akhenaten (who briefly attempted to introduce monotheism via the sun disc Aten), and Ramses II (Ramses the Great, famous for massive building projects and his war with the Hittites).

---

## 3. Ancient Greece
Not a single empire, but a collection of fiercely independent city-states (*poleis*) sharing a common language and religion.

### The Bronze Age
*   **Minoans (Crete):** A maritime trading culture known for palace complexes (Knossos) and the myth of the Minotaur.
*   **Mycenaeans (Mainland):** A more militaristic culture, the basis for Homer's *Iliad* and the legends of the Trojan War.

### The Classical Period (c. 500 – 323 BC)
*   **Athens vs. Sparta:** The two dominant powers. Athens was a naval empire focused on democracy, philosophy, and the arts. Sparta was an oligarchic, heavily militarized society.
*   **The Persian Wars:** The Greek city-states united to repel two massive invasions by the Persian Empire (famous battles: Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis).
*   **The Golden Age of Athens:** Under Pericles, Athens produced Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Aeschylus, and Sophocles, laying the foundation for Western philosophy, theater, and political thought.

### The Hellenistic Period (323 – 31 BC)
*   **Alexander the Great:** A Macedonian king who conquered the Persian Empire, spreading Greek culture from Egypt to the borders of India. Following his death, his generals carved his empire into Hellenistic kingdoms (e.g., Ptolemaic Egypt).

---

## 4. Ancient Rome
Rising from a small city on the Tiber River to dominate the entire Mediterranean world.

### The Roman Republic (509 – 27 BC)
*   **Governance:** Ruled by the Senate and two annually elected Consuls. A complex system of checks and balances.
*   **The Punic Wars:** Three massive wars against the North African empire of Carthage (led famously by Hannibal in the Second Punic War). Rome's ultimate victory gave it control of the Mediterranean.
*   **The Fall:** Income inequality, reliance on slave labor, and the rise of powerful generals (Julius Caesar) led to civil wars that destroyed the Republic.

### The Roman Empire (27 BC – 476 AD)
*   **Augustus (Octavian):** The first Emperor, establishing the *Pax Romana* (Roman Peace), a period of relative stability and massive infrastructure growth (roads, aqueducts).
*   **Engineering:** The perfection of concrete allowed Rome to build the Pantheon, the Colosseum, and spanning aqueducts that survived for millennia.
*   **The Split:** In 285 AD, the empire was divided into the Western Roman Empire (centered in Rome) and the Eastern Roman Empire (centered in Constantinople).
*   **The Fall:** The Western Empire collapsed in 476 AD due to economic instability, political corruption, and invasions by Germanic tribes (Goths, Vandals). The Eastern Empire (Byzantine Empire) survived until 1453.

## 5. Ancient China
A civilization characterized by dynastic cycles and deep philosophical traditions.

### The Shang Dynasty (c. 1600 – 1046 BC)
*   The first historically confirmed dynasty, known for exquisite bronze work, the first Chinese writing system (oracle bone script), and ancestor worship.

### The Zhou Dynasty (c. 1046 – 256 BC)
*   Introduced the **Mandate of Heaven**: the idea that the ruler was chosen by heaven, and natural disasters or rebellions were signs that heaven was withdrawing its favor.

### The Warring States Period & The Philosophers (c. 475 – 221 BC)
*   A time of brutal conflict between regional warlords, but also the "Hundred Schools of Thought."
*   **Confucianism (Confucius):** Focused on social harmony, filial piety, and moral leadership.
*   **Daoism (Laozi):** Focused on living in harmony with the *Dao* (the Way), emphasizing non-action (*wu wei*) and natural balance.

### The Qin Dynasty (221 – 206 BC)
*   **Qin Shi Huang:** The first Emperor of a unified China. He standardized weights, measures, and writing, began the Great Wall, and ordered the creation of the Terracotta Army. His rule was incredibly brutal (Legalism).
