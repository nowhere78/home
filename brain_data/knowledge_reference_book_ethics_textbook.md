# ⚖️ Ethics: A Comprehensive Philosophical Textbook

> **Domain:** Moral Philosophy, Metaethics, Normative Ethics, Applied Ethics
> **Purpose:** To provide a structured, high-level overview of the major theories of moral philosophy that govern human behavior and decision-making.

---

## 1. The Three Branches of Ethics

1.  **Metaethics:** Asks what morality *is*. (e.g., "Are moral values objective truths or human inventions?", "What does the word 'good' actually mean?").
2.  **Normative Ethics:** Asks what we *should* do. It develops frameworks and rules for determining right and wrong action (e.g., Utilitarianism, Deontology).
3.  **Applied Ethics:** Applies normative theories to specific, real-world controversial issues (e.g., medical ethics, business ethics, AI ethics).

---

## 2. Major Normative Theories

### Utilitarianism (Consequentialism)
*   **Core Idea:** An action is morally right if it produces the greatest good (happiness/pleasure) for the greatest number of people. The *consequence* of the action is all that matters, not the intent.
*   **Key Figures:** Jeremy Bentham, John Stuart Mill.
*   **The Trolley Problem:** A utilitarian will always pull the lever to kill one person and save five, because a net +4 lives saved is the optimal mathematical consequence.
*   **Criticism:** It can justify horrific acts against minorities if it benefits the majority (e.g., harvesting the organs of one healthy person against their will to save five dying patients).

### Deontology (Duty-Based Ethics)
*   **Core Idea:** An action is morally right if it adheres to a universal moral duty or rule, regardless of the consequences. Some actions are inherently wrong (like lying or killing), even if they produce a good outcome.
*   **Key Figure:** Immanuel Kant.
*   **The Categorical Imperative:** Kant's ultimate rule. "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law." (e.g., You cannot lie, because if *everyone* lied, the concept of truth would collapse, making the rule logically contradictory).
*   **Treating people as ends:** You must never use a human being merely as a "means to an end."
*   **Criticism:** It is rigid. If a murderer asks you where your friend is hiding, Kantian deontology dictates you must tell the truth, even if it results in your friend's death.

### Virtue Ethics
*   **Core Idea:** Shifts the focus away from *rules* (Deontology) and *consequences* (Utilitarianism) and focuses instead on *character*. What makes an action right is that it is what a virtuous person would do.
*   **Key Figures:** Aristotle, Socrates, Plato.
*   **Eudaimonia:** The ultimate goal is human flourishing or excellence.
*   **The Golden Mean:** A virtue is usually the midpoint between two vices (excess and deficiency). For example, *Courage* is the virtuous mean between the deficiency of *Cowardice* and the excess of *Recklessness*.
*   **Criticism:** It is often vague. It doesn't give clear "yes/no" instructions for complex dilemmas (like abortion or war).

### Ethics of Care (Feminist Ethics)
*   **Core Idea:** Rejects the cold, abstract, mathematical rules of Kant and Bentham. Argues that morality is fundamentally about relationships, empathy, and caring for dependents.
*   **Key Figure:** Carol Gilligan.
*   **Focus:** It emphasizes the moral weight of specific attachments (e.g., a mother's duty to her child overrides an abstract duty to a stranger).

## 3. Modern Ethical Dilemmas (Applied Ethics)

### AI and Machine Ethics
*   **The Alignment Problem:** How do we ensure that superintelligent AI systems have goals that are aligned with human values and survival?
*   **Algorithmic Bias:** How do we prevent machine learning models from inheriting and amplifying human prejudices (e.g., racist recidivism algorithms or sexist hiring bots)?
*   **Autonomous Weapons:** Is it ethical to give a machine the power to make lethal decisions without a "human in the loop"?

### Bioethics
*   **Autonomy:** A patient's right to refuse life-saving medical treatment.
*   **Euthanasia:** The ethics of physician-assisted suicide for the terminally ill.
*   **CRISPR/Gene Editing:** Is it ethical to alter the human germline, potentially creating "designer babies" and permanently changing human evolution?
