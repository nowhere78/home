# 🔬 Philosophy of Science: A Comprehensive Reference Guide

> **Domain:** Epistemology, Scientific Methodology, Logic, History of Science
> **Purpose:** To provide a structured overview of the foundational theories regarding what science is, how it works, and how it progresses.

---

## 1. The Demarcation Problem
The central question in the philosophy of science: *How do we distinguish between genuine science and pseudoscience?*

### Logical Positivism & Verificationism
*   **Core Idea:** Developed by the Vienna Circle in the 1920s. They argued that a statement is only cognitively meaningful if it can be empirically verified (tested through observation).
*   **The Problem:** The verification principle itself cannot be empirically verified. Furthermore, universal scientific laws (e.g., "All swans are white") can never be definitively verified, because you can never observe every single swan in the universe.

### Karl Popper & Falsificationism
*   **Core Idea:** Popper argued against Verificationism. He stated that science does not proceed by proving theories true, but by proving them false.
*   **The Criterion:** A theory is only scientific if it is *falsifiable*—meaning there must be some conceivable observation that could prove it wrong.
*   **Example:** Einstein's Theory of Relativity made risky, highly specific predictions (like light bending around the sun during an eclipse) that could have easily been proven wrong. Therefore, it is good science. Popper argued that Marxism and Freudian psychoanalysis were *pseudoscience* because they were too flexible; they could explain away any contradictory evidence, making them unfalsifiable.

## 2. How Does Science Progress?

### Thomas Kuhn & Paradigm Shifts
In his 1962 book *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, Kuhn revolutionized how we view scientific progress. He argued against the idea that science is a slow, steady accumulation of facts.
1.  **Normal Science:** Science operates within a dominant "Paradigm" (a shared set of assumptions, theories, and methodologies, like Newtonian physics). Scientists act as puzzle-solvers within this framework.
2.  **Anomalies:** Eventually, observations arise that cannot be explained by the current paradigm (e.g., the orbit of Mercury not fitting Newton's equations).
3.  **Crisis:** When anomalies accumulate, the field enters a crisis state.
4.  **Paradigm Shift (Scientific Revolution):** A radically new framework is proposed (e.g., Einstein's Relativity). It eventually overthrows the old paradigm.
*   **Incommensurability:** Kuhn controversially argued that different paradigms are conceptually incompatible. A Newtonian physicist and an Einsteinian physicist use the same words (like "mass" or "time") but mean entirely different things, making objective comparison difficult.

### Imre Lakatos & Research Programmes
Lakatos attempted to reconcile Popper's rigid falsification with Kuhn's historical paradigm shifts.
*   **Research Programme:** Instead of evaluating single theories, we evaluate entire "research programmes." These consist of a "hard core" of fundamental assumptions (which are protected from falsification) surrounded by a "protective belt" of auxiliary hypotheses (which can be modified or falsified to save the core).
*   **Progressive vs. Degenerating:** A research programme is *progressive* if modifying the protective belt leads to new, successful predictions. It is *degenerating* if modifications only exist to explain away failures (ad hoc adjustments) without generating new knowledge.

## 3. Realism vs. Anti-Realism
What is the actual status of the unobservable entities described by science (e.g., electrons, quarks, black holes)?

### Scientific Realism
*   **Core Idea:** The universe exists independently of our minds, and successful scientific theories provide a true (or approximately true) description of both the observable and *unobservable* world.
*   **The "No Miracles" Argument:** The incredible predictive success of modern science would be a "miracle" if the unobservable entities it describes did not actually exist.

### Instrumentalism (Anti-Realism)
*   **Core Idea:** Scientific theories are merely useful tools (instruments) for predicting observable phenomena. We should not assume that the unobservable entities they posit actually exist in reality.
*   **Analogy:** A quantum wavefunction is just a mathematical tool to calculate the probability of where an electron will hit a screen; it is not a literal physical wave existing in the world.

## 4. Ockham's Razor (Lex Parsimoniae)
A foundational heuristic in science, attributed to William of Ockham (14th century).
*   **The Principle:** "Entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity."
*   **Application:** When presented with competing hypotheses that make the same predictions, one should select the solution with the fewest assumptions. Simpler explanations are generally preferable to complex ones.
