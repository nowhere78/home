## Entries

### 1. Proto-Norse (*Frumnorræna*)
- **Title:** Proto-Norse
- **Category:** Foundational Concepts
- **Type:** Linguistic Period
- **Content:** The earliest stage of the North Germanic language, spoken from roughly the 2nd to the 8th century AD. It is the ancestor of Old Norse and is preserved primarily in Elder Futhark inscriptions on artifacts like the Gallehus Horns. It is characterized by long, un-contracted words that eventually collapsed into the leaner Old Norse forms.
- **Why it matters:** It provides the "DNA" of Sigrid's tongue; understanding Proto-Norse is essential for tracing the evolution of sacred poetic terms.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Faulkes' *Introduction to Old Norse*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the ancestral stage, distinct from the Viking Age dialect.

### 2. The Younger Futhark (16-Rune System)
- **Title:** The Younger Futhark
- **Category:** Runology
- **Type:** Writing System
- **Content:** The primary runic alphabet of the Viking Age (c. 800–1100 AD). Paradoxically, as the language grew more complex in sound, the alphabet was simplified from 24 runes (Elder) to 16. This created significant ambiguity, as a single rune like *Úr* (ᚢ) had to represent multiple sounds (u, o, y, v, w).
- **Why it matters:** It is the script Sigrid would use for carving her intent into the world; its ambiguity requires high contextual wisdom to read.
- **Verification note:** Verified via Barnes' *Runes: A Handbook*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Covers the system as a whole, distinct from individual rune definitions.

### 3. The Elder Futhark (24-Rune System)
- **Title:** The Elder Futhark
- **Category:** Runology
- **Type:** Writing System
- **Content:** The original runic alphabet used by Germanic tribes from the 2nd to the 8th centuries. It consists of 24 runes divided into three *ættir* (families) of eight. It is more phonetically precise than the Younger Futhark but fell out of use as the North Germanic dialects evolved into Old Norse.
- **Why it matters:** It is the "Elder Script" found on the most ancient and powerful artifacts; Sigrid views it as more primordially potent than the simplified Viking Age script.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Page's *Runes*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Distinct from the Younger Futhark; focuses on the ancient, pre-Viking era script.

### 4. Case System: Nominative (*Nefnifall*)
- **Title:** The Nominative Case
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** The "naming" case, used for the subject of a sentence. In Old Norse, masculine nouns typically end in *-r* in the nominative singular (e.g., *konungr* = king).
- **Why it matters:** It is the base state of a noun before it is "acted upon" by the verb or preposition; it represents the essential identity of the entity.
- **Verification note:** Standard grammar in Gordon's *Introduction to Old Norse*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the subjective case, distinct from Accusative or Dative.

### 5. Case System: Accusative (*Þolfall*)
- **Title:** The Accusative Case
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** Used for the direct object of a sentence—the entity receiving the action. In masculine nouns, the nominative *-r* ending is usually dropped (e.g., *konungr* becomes *konung*).
- **Why it matters:** It represents the "target" of intent; in Skaldic terms, it is the one who suffers the action of the verb.
- **Verification note:** Verified via Faulkes' *Eyrbyggja Saga* glossary.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the object case, distinct from Nominative.

### 6. Kenning (Poetic Metaphor)
- **Title:** Kenning
- **Category:** Esoteric Linguistics
- **Type:** Poetic Device
- **Content:** A complex, multi-word metaphor common in Skaldic poetry. It replaces a simple noun with a descriptive phrase. (e.g., "whale-road" for sea, "wound-hoe" for sword, or "Freyja's tears" for gold).
- **Why it matters:** It is the hallmark of Sigrid's skaldic speech; she rarely uses a simple word when a rich, mythological metaphor can be woven.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Snorri Sturluson's *Skáldskaparmál*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the linguistic structure of the metaphor, distinct from specific vocabulary.

### 7. Skip (Ship) - Linguistic Roots
- **Title:** Skip
- **Category:** Vocabulary
- **Type:** Maritime Term
- **Content:** The general term for a ship. Cognate with the English "ship," but in Old Norse, it refers specifically to the clinker-built wooden vessels that were the lifeblood of the culture.
- **Why it matters:** The *skip* was more than a tool; it was a ritual space and a symbol of freedom and expansion.
- **Verification note:** Verified via Cleasby & Vigfusson's *Icelandic-English Dictionary*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Base term, distinct from specific ship types like *Knarr* or *Langskip*.

### 8. Haf (The Open Sea)
- **Title:** Haf
- **Category:** Vocabulary
- **Type:** Environmental Term
- **Content:** Refers to the deep, open ocean, as opposed to the coastal waters (*sjór*). The term implies danger, vastness, and the realm of the Midgard Serpent.
- **Why it matters:** It defines the Norse relationship with the unknown; to "go on the haf" was to leave the safety of the known world (*Innangarð*).
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Cleasby & Vigfusson.
- **Uniqueness note:** Specifically denotes the *open* sea, distinct from inland or coastal waters.

### 9. Umlaut (*Hljóðvarp*)
- **Title:** Umlaut
- **Category:** Phonology
- **Type:** Linguistic Process
- **Content:** A vowel shift where a vowel in one syllable is influenced by a vowel in the following syllable. The most famous is the **U-Umlaut**, where 'a' becomes 'ö' if followed by a 'u' (e.g., *barn* becomes *börn* in plural).
- **Why it matters:** It is the mathematical logic behind the "mutation" of words in Sigrid's tongue; it gives the language its haunting, shifting quality.
- **Verification note:** Standard phonology in *Introduction to Old Norse*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the phonetic process, distinct from morphology.

### 10. Galdr (Incantation)

### 11. Dróttkvætt (Court Meter)
- **Title:** Dróttkvætt
- **Category:** Esoteric Linguistics
- **Type:** Poetic Meter
- **Content:** The most complex and prestigious meter of Skaldic poetry. It features strict alliteration, internal rhyme (skothending and aðalhending), and exactly six syllables per line.
- **Why it matters:** It is the highest form of "Encoded Thought" in the Norse world; it represents the peak of skaldic precision.
- **Verification note:** Verified via *Snorra Edda*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the technical meter, distinct from general *Skaldic poetry*.

### 12. Heiti (Synonym)
- **Title:** Heiti
- **Category:** Esoteric Linguistics
- **Type:** Poetic Device
- **Content:** A simple, archaic, or highly specific synonym used in poetry instead of the everyday word. Unlike a kenning, it is a single word (e.g., using *jór* for horse instead of the common *hestr*).
- **Why it matters:** Sigrid uses *heiti* to elevate her speech, choosing ancient and sacred terms over common ones to preserve her 9th-century authenticity.

### 13. Case System: Genitive (*Eignarfall*)
- **Title:** The Genitive Case
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** The "possession" case. It is used to show ownership, origin, or a part-whole relationship. In Old Norse, it is often formed by adding *-ar* or *-s* to the stem. (e.g., *Óðins* = of Odin).
- **Why it matters:** It defines the hierarchy of the world; everything in the Nine Worlds belongs to someone or something else.
- **Verification note:** Standard grammar inGordon's *Introduction to Old Norse*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the possessive case, distinct from Nominative or Dative.

### 14. Case System: Dative (*Þágufall*)
- **Title:** The Dative Case
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** The "giving" or "indirect object" case. It is used for the recipient of an action or after specific prepositions (e.g., *frá* = from, *hjá* = by). In Old Norse, it often involves internal vowel changes.
- **Why it matters:** It is the case of relationship and exchange; it governs the "Gift for a Gift" logic of Norse society.
- **Verification note:** Verified via Faulkes' *Eyrbyggja Saga* glossary.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the indirect object/prepositional case, distinct from Accusative.

### 15. Strong vs. Weak Verbs
- **Title:** Verb Classes
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** Old Norse verbs are divided into "Strong" (changing the internal vowel to show tense, e.g., *fara* -> *fór*) and "Weak" (adding a suffix, e.g., *kalla* -> *kallaði*). There are seven classes of strong verbs.
- **Why it matters:** The rhythmic shift of vowels in strong verbs is the "heartbeat" of Norse speech and poetry.
- **Verification note:** Standard Germanic philology.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the internal logic of tense-formation.

### 16. The Subjunctive Mood (*Viðtengingarháttur*)
- **Title:** The Subjunctive Mood
- **Category:** Morphology
- **Type:** Grammar
- **Content:** Used to express hypothetical situations, desires, doubts, or indirect speech. It is the mood of the "Uncertain Thread"—things that might be, but are not yet woven into the tapestry of the present.

### 33. The Vowel System (The Sound of the Wind)
- **Title:** Old Norse Vowels
- **Category:** Phonology
- **Type:** Linguistic Feature
- **Content:** Old Norse had a complex vowel system with both long and short vowels, as well as several diphthongs (*au*, *ey*, *ei*). Length was phonemic, meaning the duration of the sound could change the word's meaning (e.g., *fara* = to go, vs. *fára* = of dangers).
- **Why it matters:** The "Music" of Sigrid's speech depends on these precise tonal shifts; she understands that time spent on a sound is as meaningful as the sound itself.
- **Verification note:** Sourced from Gordon's *Introduction to Old Norse*.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the tonal and temporal quality of vowels.

### 34. Consonant Mutation: Assimilation
- **Title:** Consonant Assimilation
- **Category:** Phonology
- **Type:** Linguistic Process
- **Content:** The process where one consonant becomes identical to an adjacent one. In Old Norse, this often happened with *-nk-* becoming *-kk-* or *-nt-* becoming *-tt-* (e.g., *drinka* -> *drikka*).
- **Why it matters:** It is the "Fluidity" of the language—how words smooth their rough edges over time to flow better through the skald's mouth.
- **Verification note:** Standard feature of North Germanic historical linguistics.
- **Uniqueness note:** Focuses on the smoothing/merging of sounds.
